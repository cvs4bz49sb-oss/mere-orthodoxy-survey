Mere Orthodoxy Reader Survey | 2026

381 respondents · 281 subscribers · 100 members · February 2026

The Big Picture

381 readers responded to the February 2026 survey: 281 subscribers and 100 paying members. Together, they paint a remarkably coherent picture of who MO serves and what it should do next.

Your readership is 76-78% male, heavily Protestant (93%), and disproportionately composed of pastors and elders (36% of subscribers, 36% of members). No single denomination exceeds 22% among subscribers, though Presbyterians dominate the paying member base at 38%. The "mere" in Mere Orthodoxy is genuinely working for your free audience; your paying audience skews Reformed.

What Readers Want

Three topics form the universal foundation: Theology (80%), Culture (77%), and Church (75%). These are consistent across every age group, gender, and role. A crucial insight emerges from the member data: members want theological depth (52%) and cultural commentary (50%) in nearly equal measure. Both groups want depth applied to culture. The answer is not to choose; it is to apply theological depth to cultural questions.

The most significant content gap remains Family. 63% of 25-34 year olds want it, members with young children cite parenting as their dominant challenge, and it currently ranks 7th in your content mix. Technology is the emerging topic, with 53-56% interest among under-35 readers and rising.

What They Are Struggling With

Across 278 free-text responses from both groups, the pattern is unmistakable: time and competing demands, grief over the politicization of evangelicalism, the strain of parenting in a hostile culture, and the loneliness of trying to think seriously about faith. Among members specifically, time constraints have emerged as the dominant challenge, with political polarization close behind. Among subscribers, political polarization runs deepest.

Pastors report a distinctive profile across both datasets: political polarization, leadership strain, loneliness, and ecclesial identity questions. They are your most loyal cohort and came to MO for intellectual companionship they cannot find elsewhere.

A new signal from members deserves attention: multiple members explicitly criticize publishing volume, asking for fewer but higher-quality articles. As one member put it, prioritizing quantity over quality dilutes the brand.

What You Should Do

Reconsider your publishing volume. Members are telling you directly that two articles per day is too many. They would rather receive fewer pieces that are truly excellent. This is your most committed audience speaking. Listen to them.

Deepen both theology and cultural commentary. Members want theological depth (52%) and cultural commentary (50%) in nearly equal measure. The sweet spot is both: sustained theological analysis applied to cultural questions.

Launch a Faith & Family content vertical. This is the single biggest unmet need across both datasets. Frame it as the intersection of formation, ecclesiology, and practical wisdom.

Protect and invest in the Journal. 45% of members cite it as a reason they joined. It is your most tangible membership benefit and your clearest differentiator from free content.

Invest in Technology & AI coverage. Frame it as theological-cultural analysis: what does it mean to be human in a digital age.

Tend the Discord community intentionally. 10% of members joined specifically for it, but feedback is mixed. Insiders enjoy it; newcomers feel excluded. Online communities require active cultivation, not autopilot.

Pilot small regional events. 41% of 25-34 subscribers and 28% of members want them. Start with dinner discussions in Texas, Michigan, and the Carolinas.

Address the political pain directly. Your readers don't want you to take a side. They want you to model faithful engagement that transcends the partisan binary.

What Not to Do

Only 4.6% want more social media. Only 5-8% want video. Your readers are readers. Your competitive moat is depth, not reach. The path to growth is not broadening — it is deepening your existing strengths while widening the aperture to include family, technology, and community.

Fundraising Summary

This summary focuses on membership revenue, donor conversion, retention strategy, and the financial sustainability questions raised by the survey data.

The Donor Profile

Your 100 paying members represent roughly 36% of your subscriber base. They are your most engaged readers (85% weekly vs. 70%), slightly younger (47% under 45 vs. 43%), and disproportionately Presbyterian (38% vs. 22%). The critical fundraising insight: these are values-driven donors, not transactional consumers. 60% say they joined to support the mission. 65% cite your theological and cultural perspectives as their primary motivation. They pay because they believe in what you are doing — not because of any specific perk.

The Conversion Funnel

The data reveals a clear path from free reader to paying member: Substack or TGC discovery → regular website reading → Mere Fidelity listening → membership conversion. Each stage can be optimized for fundraising:

  • Discovery: Substack indexes highest among under-35s (43-44%). It is your primary acquisition channel for the next generation of donors.
  • Deepening: Mere Fidelity is your most effective conversion bridge. Members use it at 36% vs. 26% of subscribers. The parasocial connection podcasts create translates directly into financial commitment.
  • Conversion trigger: Mission alignment (60%) + theological perspectives (65%) + the Journal (45%). Lead fundraising appeals with values and voice, not features.
  • Retention: Editorial quality is the primary retention lever. Members who feel the quality is declining are your highest churn risk.

Revenue Concentration Risk

Presbyterians are 38% of paying members but only 22% of subscribers. This 16-point gap means your revenue base is narrower than your audience. Baptists are 20% of subscribers but only 17% of members — your biggest untapped denominational conversion pool. Non-denominational readers convert at parity (19%/19%), proving that broader appeal is possible. Diversifying the paying base without diluting what Presbyterians love is the key fundraising challenge.

The Journal as Fundraising Asset

45% of members cite the print Journal as a reason they joined. Physical media creates a fundamentally different relationship with a publication — it sits on nightstands and coffee tables, gets shared with friends, and signals intellectual identity. The Journal is not a cost center. It is your most effective membership conversion and retention tool. Increasing its quality (better paper, exclusive content, themed issues) could justify a higher membership price point.

Membership Benefits That Matter

What drives membership revenue, ranked: theological perspectives (65%), mission support (60%), the Journal (45%), Mere Fidelity (28%), Discord (10%), Christians Reading Classics (7%). The top three are all about the content and mission. Discord and CRC are niche benefits that matter deeply to small segments but don't drive overall revenue. Don't over-invest in perks at the expense of the editorial quality that actually generates donations.

Growth Opportunities

Regional events could become a fundraising vehicle. 28% of members and 41% of 25-34 subscribers want in-person gatherings. Ticketed dinner discussions or one-day conferences could generate revenue while deepening donor relationships. Start in Texas, Michigan, and North Carolina where readers are concentrated.

The quality-over-quantity signal has fundraising implications. Members are explicitly saying they'd prefer fewer, better articles. If reducing publishing volume improves quality and reduces costs simultaneously, it could improve both retention and margins.

Editorial Summary

This summary focuses on content strategy, editorial priorities, and audience needs surfaced by the survey data.

The Content Hierarchy

The universal triad — Theology, Culture, and Church — remains your foundation (71-84% across both groups). Everything else is important but secondary. The critical editorial insight is that these three are not separate buckets. Your best content lives at their intersection, and that's what readers are asking for: theological depth applied to cultural questions in the context of church life.

The Culture-Theology Balance

Subscribers want more theological depth (57%). Members want theological depth (52%) and cultural commentary (50%) in nearly equal measure — not the cultural commentary dominance seen in the earlier sample. This is an important recalibration. Your members don't simply want cultural commentary instead of theology; they want both, deeply integrated. The editorial strategy: continue deepening theological content (it's your acquisition engine) while increasing timely cultural commentary (it's your retention engine). The sweet spot is theology applied to culture.

Quality Over Quantity

A new and significant signal from the member data: multiple members explicitly criticize the current publishing pace. They report a perceived decline in article quality with the move to two articles per day. Recycled Substack posts and shorter, shallower articles are not what they read MO for. They want fewer articles of greater depth and quality. This is actionable editorial feedback from your most committed readers.

Content Gaps to Fill

Family. 63% of 25-34 year olds want it. Members with young children cite parenting as a top challenge. This is not "mommy blog" territory — it's the intersection of formation, ecclesiology, and practical wisdom. How do you raise children to see Jesus in a church that has become indistinguishable from partisan politics? That's an MO question.

Technology & AI. Interest runs 53-56% among under-35 readers. It was the second-most mentioned challenge theme at 19%. Your younger readers are grappling with what it means to be embodied creatures in a digital age. This is a natural extension of your formation and cultural commentary strengths.

Timely cultural commentary. Not just evergreen theological reflection, but engagement with cultural moments — books, films, trends, events. Consider a weekly or biweekly cultural commentary column.

Content to Protect

Long-form theological depth. 57% of subscribers and 52% of members want more. This is your competitive moat. Consider multi-part series and sustained engagement with single topics over weeks — but ensure each piece justifies its publication.

Book reviews. 47-62% interest across both groups. Your readership is a reading readership. Book reviews drive engagement and differentiate you from outlets that only react to news.

The Political Voice

13.3% of subscribers explicitly named political polarization as their top challenge. Among pastors, 17.6%. The overwhelming undercurrent across hundreds of free-text responses is grief over what partisan politics has done to the evangelical church. Your readers don't want you to take a side. They want you to model faithful engagement that transcends the binary. Lean into this more explicitly — it's exactly your brand.

Pathways for Women Readers

Women are 22-24% of readership and most are lay members. They report that most women's ministry lacks theological depth. Women want Family content at higher rates and report parenting challenges more frequently. Recruit women contributors. Address the theological depth gap in women's ministry. This is both a growth opportunity and a moral imperative for a publication called "Mere Orthodoxy."

Marketing Summary

This summary focuses on audience growth, member acquisition, competitive positioning, and channel strategy.

Your Competitive Position

MO occupies a distinctive niche: more intellectual than TGC, more Protestant than First Things, less institutionally cautious than CT, and more editorial than individual Substacks. 51% of your readers also follow TGC, 46% read Substack newsletters, 37% follow CT, and 33% follow First Things. This positioning is your strategic advantage — guard it.

Growth Demographics

Your under-35 cohort (24.5% of subscribers) is disproportionately engaged and hungry for cultural commentary, family content, and community. They represent your future. However, the updated member data shows a shift: 47% of members are under 45 (vs. 43% of subscribers). While younger readers still convert at slightly higher rates, the gap has narrowed with the larger sample. The 55+ cohort now represents 40% of members, indicating strong support from older readers as well.

The Gavin Ortlund Effect

24% of all readers follow Gavin Ortlund/Truth Unites, but among under-25s it's 44% and among 25-34s it's 47%. He is the single biggest gateway figure for your younger audience. Cross-pollination — guest appearances, shared events, co-authored pieces — could significantly boost reach with your growth demographic.

The Acquisition Funnel

The data reveals a clear optimal member conversion path: Substack or TGC crossover → regular website reader → Mere Fidelity listener → membership conversion. Each step can be optimized:

  • Discovery: Invest in Substack presence. It indexes highest among under-35 readers (43-44%). Use its community features to build belonging.
  • Engagement: Mere Fidelity is a conversion engine. Members use it at 36% vs. 26% of subscribers. The parasocial relationship podcasts create is powerful.
  • Conversion: Mission alignment + Journal + theological perspectives. Lead with values, not perks. Members' #1 motivation is theological perspectives (65%), followed by mission support (60%).
  • Retention: Editorial quality, Discord community, Journal. Values-driven members stay for the voice. But heed the quality-over-quantity feedback — publishing too much can erode the brand that attracted them.

The Presbyterian Concentration

Presbyterians are 22% of subscribers but 38% of members. This is your deepest financial support well, but it's also a concentration risk. The strategic question: can you broaden your paying base without diluting what Presbyterians love about you? The Baptist gap (20% of subscribers, 17% of members) is your biggest untapped pool. Non-denominational readers convert well at 19%/19%.

What Not to Invest In

Only 4.6% want social media engagement. Only 5-8% want video. Only 9-12% want online events. Your readers are readers. The marketing strategy is not broader reach — it is deeper engagement with people who already want what you offer. Word of mouth among theologically serious Christians is your most effective growth channel. Make the content so good that pastors share it with their congregations and professors assign it to their students.

The Secular-Adjacent Audience

Members are more likely to follow The Dispatch and other secular-adjacent publications. Meaningful minorities follow The Free Press (14%) and UnHerd (10%). Content that bridges Christian thought and mainstream intellectual conversation — without compromising theological commitments — would especially serve and retain these readers. This is a growing segment and a natural fit for MO's cultural commentary ambitions.

Strategic Recommendations for Members

High Priority

1. Reconsider Publishing Volume

Members are directly criticizing the two-articles-per-day pace. They want fewer, higher-quality pieces. This is your most committed audience telling you that quantity is diluting your brand. Consider reducing to the best 2-3 articles per week that truly justify their time.

High Priority

2. Deepen Both Theology and Cultural Commentary

52% want theological depth, 50% want cultural commentary. The sweet spot is both: sustained theological analysis applied to cultural questions. This is what MO does at its best. Do more of it.

High Priority

3. Protect and Invest in the Journal

45% cite the Journal as a reason they joined. Consider higher quality paper/design, more frequent issues, or exclusive content that doesn't appear online. It is your primary member retention tool.

Medium Priority

4. Launch Small Regional Gatherings

28% of members want regional events. Start with 2-3 pilot events in concentrated areas. A dinner discussion, a reading group, or a one-day mini-conference. Partner with local churches or seminaries.

Medium Priority

5. Invest in Discord Intentionally

19% of members use Discord, and 10% joined for it. But feedback is mixed — insiders enjoy it, newcomers feel excluded. Add structured programming: weekly discussion threads, reading groups, AMAs, and visible editorial presence. Treat it as a real community product.

Medium Priority

6. Broaden Denominational Appeal

Presbyterians are 38% of members vs. 22% of subscribers. Baptists are 17% of members vs. 20% of subscribers. Non-denominational readers convert well at 19%/19%. Consider Baptist and broader evangelical voices to widen the paying base without diluting what Presbyterians love.

Medium Priority

7. Use Mere Fidelity as a Conversion Funnel

36% of members engage via Mere Fidelity (vs. 26% of subscribers). Consider occasional calls-to-action on the podcast and listener-exclusive content behind the membership paywall.

Lower Priority

8. Create Content for the Technology Conversation

43% of members are interested in Technology (vs. 35% of subscribers). Frame it as theological anthropology for a digital age. Your members are already thinking about these questions — give them the depth they expect from MO.

The Retention Equation. Your members are values-driven, not perks-driven. Retention depends on editorial quality and voice. Don't over-engineer the membership. Keep the Journal excellent, make the community genuine, and above all — heed their plea for quality over quantity. Fewer excellent articles will retain members better than many mediocre ones.

The Reader Journey

The combined data reveals a clear pathway from discovery to commitment:

Stage 1: Discovery. Readers find MO via Substack (indexes highest among under-35s at 43-44%), TGC crossover (51% overlap), or Gavin Ortlund (44% of under-25s follow him). The entry point is typically a single article that resonates.

Stage 2: Regular Reading. They migrate to the website (69-74%) and newsletter (48-51%). Reading frequency increases to multiple times per week. They begin to trust the editorial voice.

Stage 3: Audio Engagement. They discover Mere Fidelity. Members use it at 36% vs. 26% of subscribers. The parasocial connection to MO's editorial voice deepens commitment. This is the bridge between reading and belonging.

Stage 4: Conversion. They become paying members. The triggers: theological perspectives (65%), mission alignment (60%), and the Journal (45%). Substack usage drops (20% vs. 32%) as they commit to the direct platform.

Stage 5: Community. The frontier. 19% of members use Discord, 28% want regional events. This stage is nascent and under-built. Whether MO can create genuine intellectual community — not just publish content — may determine the next chapter of its growth.

Where Both Groups Agree

  • The Universal Triad. Theology + Culture + Church are the foundation across both groups (71-84%). This should never change.
  • Family is underserved. 39-41% interest, rising to 63% among 25-34 year olds. The clearest content gap in the data.
  • Technology is emerging. 35-43% interest, but 53-56% among under-35s. The topic of the next decade.
  • Political polarization is the shared wound. Among the most cited personal challenges across age groups, roles, and membership status.
  • They want depth, not breadth. Only 5-6% want social media. Only 5-8% want video. Your readers are readers.

Where They Diverge

  • Theology vs. Culture priority. Subscribers want more theological depth (57%); members want both theological depth (52%) and cultural commentary (50%) nearly equally. The earlier cultural-commentary-dominant pattern has moderated with the larger sample.
  • Substack vs. direct. Subscribers use Substack more (32% vs. 20%). Members have committed to the direct platform. Substack is acquisition; website is retention.
  • Regional events. Members want them more (28% vs. 19%). Paying readers want belonging, not just content.
  • Denominational composition. Subscribers are evenly spread; members are disproportionately Presbyterian (38%). Non-denominational readers convert at parity (19%/19%). The "mere" works for free readers; the paying base skews Reformed.
  • Quality concerns. Members are more vocal about publishing volume, explicitly asking for fewer but better articles. This feedback is absent from the subscriber data.
