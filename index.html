Mere Orthodoxy Reader Survey | 2026

342 respondents · 281 subscribers · 61 members · February 2026

The Big Picture

342 readers responded to the February 2026 survey: 281 subscribers and 61 paying members. Together, they paint a remarkably coherent picture of who MO serves and what it should do next.

Your readership is 76-82% male, heavily Protestant (93%), and disproportionately composed of pastors and elders (36% of subscribers, 44% of members). No single denomination exceeds 22% among subscribers, though Presbyterians dominate the paying member base at 41%. The "mere" in Mere Orthodoxy is genuinely working for your free audience; your paying audience skews Reformed.

What Readers Want

Three topics form the universal foundation: Theology (80%), Culture (77%), and Church (75%). These are consistent across every age group, gender, and role. But a crucial divergence emerges between subscribers and members: subscribers want more theological depth (57%), while members — who already have the depth — want more cultural commentary (53%). Both groups are right. The answer is not to choose; it is to apply theological depth to cultural questions.

The most significant content gap remains Family. 63% of 25-34 year olds want it, members with young children cite parenting as their dominant challenge, and it currently ranks 7th in your content mix. Technology is the emerging topic, with 53-56% interest among under-35 readers and rising.

What They Are Struggling With

Across 245 free-text responses from both groups, the pattern is unmistakable: grief over the politicization of evangelicalism, the strain of parenting in a hostile culture, and the loneliness of trying to think seriously about faith. Among members specifically, parenting and family challenges dominate — roughly 1 in 4 member responses name it. Among subscribers, political polarization runs deepest.

Pastors report a distinctive profile across both datasets: political polarization, leadership strain, loneliness, and ecclesial identity questions. They are your most loyal cohort and came to MO for intellectual companionship they cannot find elsewhere.

What You Should Do

Launch a Faith & Family content vertical. This is the single biggest unmet need across both datasets. Frame it as the intersection of formation, ecclesiology, and practical wisdom.

Increase cultural commentary output. Your members are asking for it explicitly (53%), and they are your most committed readers. Apply your theological depth to the culture around you — not just evergreen reflection, but timely engagement.

Protect and invest in the Journal. 48% of members cite it as a reason they joined. It is your most tangible membership benefit and your clearest differentiator from free content.

Invest in Technology & AI coverage. Frame it as theological-cultural analysis: what does it mean to be human in a digital age.

Tend the Discord community intentionally. 15% of members joined specifically for it. Online communities require active cultivation, not autopilot.

Pilot small regional events. 41% of 25-34 subscribers and 30% of members want them. Start with dinner discussions in Texas, Michigan, and the Carolinas.

Address the political pain directly. Your readers don't want you to take a side. They want you to model faithful engagement that transcends the partisan binary.

What Not to Do

Only 4.6% want more social media. Only 7.8% want video. Your readers are readers. Your competitive moat is depth, not reach. The path to growth is not broadening — it is deepening your existing strengths while widening the aperture to include family, technology, and community.

Operations Summary

This summary focuses on the operational infrastructure, community management, and membership economics questions raised by the survey data.

Membership Conversion & Retention

Members represent roughly 22% of your subscriber base. They are younger (52% under 45 vs. 43%), more engaged (85% weekly vs. 70%), and more likely to be pastors or elders (44% vs. 36%). The conversion profile is clear: readers who become members tend to be multi-channel users who discovered MO via Substack or TGC, started reading regularly on the website, began listening to Mere Fidelity, and then converted — motivated by mission alignment, the Journal, and cultural commentary.

Members are values-driven, not perks-driven. 61% say they joined to support the mission. This means retention depends primarily on editorial quality and voice, not on accumulating features. Don't over-engineer the membership.

Community Infrastructure

Discord: 25% of members use it, and 15% cited it as a reason they joined. At least one member specifically expressed disappointment with its current state. This is a retention risk. Discord needs structured programming: weekly discussion threads, reading groups, periodic AMAs with contributors, and visible editorial presence. Treat it as a community product, not an afterthought.

Regional Events: 30% of members and 41% of 25-34 subscribers want in-person gatherings. Start with 2-3 pilot events in concentrated areas (Texas, Michigan, North Carolina). Partner with local churches or seminaries to share costs. These don't need to be large or expensive — they need to be genuine and intellectually substantive.

Channel Strategy

The website remains the primary channel (69-80%), followed by newsletter (47-48%), Substack (21-32%), and Mere Fidelity (26-38%). Key operational insight: Substack is an acquisition channel, not a retention channel. Members use it less (21% vs. 32%) because they've already committed to your direct platform. The path is: Substack discovery → regular reader → website/newsletter → paying member.

Mere Fidelity indexes 12 points higher among members (38% vs. 26%). Podcast listeners convert at a higher rate. Consider occasional membership calls-to-action on the podcast and listener-exclusive content behind the paywall.

The Journal

48% of members cite the print Journal as a reason they joined. For a digital publication, this is a strong signal. Physical media sits on nightstands and coffee tables, gets shared, and signals identity. The Journal is not a cost center — it is your primary member retention tool. Invest in its quality: higher quality paper/design, more frequent issues, or exclusive content that doesn't appear online.

The Baptist Gap

Baptists are 20% of subscribers but only 13-16% of members. This is your biggest untapped denominational conversion pool. Understanding why Baptists read but don't pay could unlock meaningful revenue. Consider Baptist voices in the podcast and Baptist theologians in articles.

Editorial Summary

This summary focuses on content strategy, editorial priorities, and audience needs surfaced by the survey data.

The Content Hierarchy

The universal triad — Theology, Culture, and Church — remains your foundation (75-87% across both groups). Everything else is important but secondary. The critical editorial insight is that these three are not separate buckets. Your best content lives at their intersection, and that's what readers are asking for: theological depth applied to cultural questions in the context of church life.

The Culture-Theology Tension

Subscribers want more theological depth (57%). Members want more cultural commentary (53%). This is not a contradiction. Your members already have theological depth — they're 44% pastors and elders. What they want is MO to apply that depth to culture around them. The editorial strategy: continue deepening theological content (it's your acquisition engine) while increasing timely cultural commentary (it's your retention engine).

Content Gaps to Fill

Family. 63% of 25-34 year olds want it. Members with young children cite parenting as their dominant challenge. This is not "mommy blog" territory — it's the intersection of formation, ecclesiology, and practical wisdom. How do you raise children to see Jesus in a church that has become indistinguishable from partisan politics? That's an MO question.

Technology & AI. Interest runs 53-56% among under-35 readers. It was the second-most mentioned challenge theme at 19%. Your younger readers are grappling with what it means to be embodied creatures in a digital age. This is a natural extension of your formation and cultural commentary strengths.

Timely cultural commentary. Not just evergreen theological reflection, but engagement with cultural moments — books, films, trends, events. Consider a weekly or biweekly cultural commentary column.

Content to Protect

Long-form theological depth. 57% want more, rising to 76% among academics. This is your competitive moat. Consider multi-part series and sustained engagement with single topics over weeks.

Book reviews. 47-51% interest across both groups. Your readership is a reading readership. Book reviews drive engagement and differentiate you from outlets that only react to news.

The Political Voice

13.3% of subscribers explicitly named political polarization as their top challenge. Among pastors, 17.6%. The overwhelming undercurrent across hundreds of free-text responses is grief over what partisan politics has done to the evangelical church. Your readers don't want you to take a side. They want you to model faithful engagement that transcends the binary. Lean into this more explicitly — it's exactly your brand.

Pathways for Women Readers

Women are 18-24% of readership and 82% are lay members. They report that most women's ministry lacks theological depth. Women want Family content at 1.5x the rate of men and report parenting challenges at 4x the rate. Recruit women contributors. Address the theological depth gap in women's ministry. This is both a growth opportunity and a moral imperative for a publication called "Mere Orthodoxy."

Marketing Summary

This summary focuses on audience growth, member acquisition, competitive positioning, and channel strategy.

Your Competitive Position

MO occupies a distinctive niche: more intellectual than TGC, more Protestant than First Things, less institutionally cautious than CT, and more editorial than individual Substacks. 51% of your readers also follow TGC, 46% read Substack newsletters, 37% follow CT, and 33% follow First Things. This positioning is your strategic advantage — guard it.

Growth Demographics

Your under-35 cohort (24.5% of subscribers) is disproportionately engaged and hungry for cultural commentary, family content, and community. They represent your future. Crucially, your younger readers convert to paying members at a higher rate than older ones — 52% of members are under 45 vs. 43% of subscribers. This contradicts the usual pattern in media and suggests your content genuinely resonates with younger audiences at a level that motivates financial commitment.

The Gavin Ortlund Effect

24% of all readers follow Gavin Ortlund/Truth Unites, but among under-25s it's 44% and among 25-34s it's 47%. He is the single biggest gateway figure for your younger audience. Cross-pollination — guest appearances, shared events, co-authored pieces — could significantly boost reach with your growth demographic.

The Acquisition Funnel

The data reveals a clear optimal member conversion path: Substack or TGC crossover → regular website reader → Mere Fidelity listener → membership conversion. Each step can be optimized:

  • Discovery: Invest in Substack presence. It indexes highest among under-35 readers (43-44%). Use its community features to build belonging.
  • Engagement: Mere Fidelity is a conversion engine. Members use it at 38% vs. 26% of subscribers. The parasocial relationship podcasts create is powerful.
  • Conversion: Mission alignment + Journal + cultural commentary promise. Lead with values, not perks.
  • Retention: Editorial quality, Discord community, Journal. Values-driven members stay for the voice.

The Presbyterian Concentration

Presbyterians are 22% of subscribers but 41% of members. This is your deepest financial support well, but it's also a concentration risk. The strategic question: can you broaden your paying base without diluting what Presbyterians love about you? The Baptist gap (20% of subscribers, 13% of members) is your biggest untapped pool.

What Not to Invest In

Only 4.6% want social media engagement. Only 7.8% want video. Only 9.3% want online events. Your readers are readers. The marketing strategy is not broader reach — it is deeper engagement with people who already want what you offer. Word of mouth among theologically serious Christians is your most effective growth channel. Make the content so good that pastors share it with their congregations and professors assign it to their students.

The Secular-Adjacent Audience

Members are 13 points more likely to follow The Dispatch (34% vs. 21%). Meaningful minorities follow The Free Press (14%) and UnHerd (10%). Content that bridges Christian thought and mainstream intellectual conversation — without compromising theological commitments — would especially serve and retain these readers. This is a growing segment and a natural fit for MO's cultural commentary ambitions.

The Reader Journey

The combined data reveals a clear pathway from discovery to commitment:

Stage 1: Discovery. Readers find MO via Substack (indexes highest among under-35s at 43-44%), TGC crossover (51% overlap), or Gavin Ortlund (44% of under-25s follow him). The entry point is typically a single article that resonates.

Stage 2: Regular Reading. They migrate to the website (69-78%) and newsletter (47-48%). Reading frequency increases to multiple times per week. They begin to trust the editorial voice.

Stage 3: Audio Engagement. They discover Mere Fidelity. Members use it at 38% vs. 26% of subscribers. The parasocial connection to MO's editorial voice deepens commitment. This is the bridge between reading and belonging.

Stage 4: Conversion. They become paying members. The triggers: mission alignment (61%), theological perspectives (64%), and the Journal (48%). Substack usage drops (22% vs. 32%) as they commit to the direct platform.

Stage 5: Community. The frontier. 25% of members use Discord, 30% want regional events. This stage is nascent and under-built. Whether MO can create genuine intellectual community — not just publish content — may determine the next chapter of its growth.

Where Both Groups Agree

  • The Universal Triad. Theology + Culture + Church are the foundation across both groups (75-87%). This should never change.
  • Family is underserved. 41-44% interest, rising to 63% among 25-34 year olds. The clearest content gap in the data.
  • Technology is emerging. 35-39% interest, but 53-56% among under-35s. The topic of the next decade.
  • Political polarization is the shared wound. The most cited personal challenge across age groups, roles, and membership status.
  • They want depth, not breadth. Only 5% want social media. Only 8% want video. Your readers are readers.

Where They Diverge

  • Theology vs. Culture priority. Subscribers want more theological depth (57%); members want more cultural commentary (53%). Both are right. Members already have the depth — they want application.
  • Substack vs. direct. Subscribers use Substack more (32% vs. 22%). Members have committed to the direct platform. Substack is acquisition; website is retention.
  • Regional events. Members want them significantly more (30% vs. 19%). Paying readers want belonging, not just content.
  • Denominational composition. Subscribers are evenly spread; members are disproportionately Presbyterian (41%). The "mere" works for free readers; the paying base skews Reformed.
  • Age profile. Members are younger (52% under 45 vs. 43%). Your younger readers are more likely to pay — an unusual and encouraging pattern.
